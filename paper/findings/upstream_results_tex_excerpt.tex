\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
 
\subsection{Recall tasks share a common retrieval-head pool}
\label{sec:result-shared}
 
QRScore detection produces a top-$K$ head set per task. Within each model,
these sets substantially overlap across tasks: pairwise top-$K=64$ Jaccard
similarity ranges from 31--80\% in Llama, 44--83\% in Qwen, and 59--77\% in
Mistral. Recall tasks draw from a shared pool of retrieval heads rather than
relying on task-isolated circuits. We characterize the structure of this
shared pool through cross-task ablation: for every (source task $s$, target
task $t$, knockout size $K$) tuple, we ablate source $s$'s top-$K$ heads and
record accuracy on target $t$. We summarize the resulting $8 \times 8$ drop
matrix at each $K$ through two task-level statistics: \emph{target
sensitivity} (the column mean: a target's mean drop across all source-task
ablations, including its own) and \emph{source efficacy} (the off-diagonal
row mean: a source's mean drop on the seven other target tasks).
 
\subsection{Target-task sensitivity is conserved across model families}
\label{sec:result-sensitivity}
 
Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity} reports heatmaps for cross-task sensitivity of
the three models. For all models, the four most sensitive tasks are
\texttt{employees\_count\_total} (0.842), \texttt{ceo\_lastname} (0.745),
\texttt{incorporation\_year} (0.682), and \texttt{incorporation\_state}
(0.629); the four least sensitive tasks have drop within 0.42--0.58. More
striking than the magnitude gap is that the most sensitive tasks are
consistent across models: \texttt{employees\_count\_total} is among the two
most sensitive tasks in 19 of the 21 $(K,\text{model})$ cells, and
\texttt{headquarters\_state} is among the four most sensitive tasks in none
of the cells.

\subsection{Source-head efficacy is not conserved across model families}
\label{sec:result-asymmetry}
 
Across model pairs, target sensitivity is $5$--$10\times$ more conserved than
source efficacy (Table~\ref{tab:kfe}). After residualizing each measure
against within-$K$ task means to remove the trivial monotonic effect of $K$
on accuracy drop, sensitivity correlates across model pairs at
$R^2 = 0.18$--$0.59$ while efficacy correlates at $R^2 = 0.02$--$0.15$.
The same target tasks are systematically fragile across model families even
when the source tasks driving the disruption differ. The identity of tasks
that collapse under ablation is model-independent; the identity of tasks
whose head ablation impacts other tasks is not.
 
The fragile and robust groups are not separated by the type of information
requested. Each group contains a name (\texttt{ceo\_lastname} /
\texttt{registrant\_name}), a state (\texttt{incorporation\_state} /
\texttt{headquarters\_state}), and a numeric value
(\texttt{employees\_count\_total} / \texttt{holder\_record\_amount}).
What feature of the task structure determines fragility is therefore not
captured by answer type. We discuss possible explanations in
Section~\ref{sec:disc}.

\paragraph{\texttt{holder\_record\_amount} is an outlier.}
The \texttt{holder\_record\_amount} row in Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity} is
visibly the lightest across all three models; it is neither broadly
disrupted by other tasks' ablations (low sensitivity) nor consistently
disruptive across model families. Two interpretations are consistent with
this pattern: the task may use a distributed retrieval strategy not
captured by current head-identification methods, or it may rely on heads
outside the shared pool the other seven tasks draw from. A measurement
caveat applies in Mistral, where \texttt{holder\_record\_amount} has a low
baseline accuracy ($0.25$) that bounds the observable drop. The same
low-sensitivity pattern holds in Llama and Qwen, where the baseline is
$\geq 0.54$, so the outlier status is not solely a baseline artifact.
 
\subsection{High overlap pairs reflect detection-protocol confounds}
\label{sec:result-overlap}
 
While the average top-$K$ Jaccard overlap among head sets across the eight
tasks is between 50 and 90 percent, two task pairs have a top-$K$ overlap of
75 to 95 percent: \texttt{headquarters\_city} with
\texttt{headquarters\_state}, and \texttt{incorporation\_state} with
\texttt{incorporation\_year}. In each of these cases, the two tasks share
the same evidence sentences (for example, ``headquartered in Malvern,
Pennsylvania'' contains the answer to both \texttt{headquarters\_city} and
\texttt{headquarters\_state}). The high observed overlap therefore reflects
the structure of the detection setup, in which QRScore converges on the same
heads when the evidence span is identical, rather than any sharing of head
sets within the model itself. We flag these pairs as confounds in the overlap
analysis; our sensitivity claims, which are derived from column-mean
structure across all eight tasks, are unaffected.
 
\subsection{Cross-dataset transfer is paradigm-specific}
\label{sec:result-paradigm}
 
Three different sources of head sets were evaluated for their impact on
performance on the SEC tasks (see Figure~\ref{fig:cross-dataset}): the head
sets detected on the SEC dataset itself (QRScore-SEC), head sets detected on
the LongMemEval dataset~\cite{wu2024longmemeval} (QRScore-LME), and head sets
detected on the Natural Questions dataset~\cite{kwiatkowski2019natural}
(QRScore-NQ), in addition to a baseline composed of three random seeds of
randomly chosen head sets.
 
\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{images/LME_NQ_SEC.png}
\caption{\textbf{LME heads transfer to SEC; NQ heads do not.} Accuracy on the
192-instance SEC test set as a function of $K$ on Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct.
QRScore-SEC and QRScore-LME both collapse to comparably low accuracy by
$K{=}32$; QRScore-NQ remains within the random-baseline 95\% CI through
$K{=}16$.}
\label{fig:cross-dataset}
\end{figure}
 
\paragraph{Results of head-set ablation on SEC tasks
(Figure~\ref{fig:cross-dataset}).} For the 192 instances of the SEC
benchmark, accuracy on the test set is reported at each value of $K$ on
Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct. The head sets detected on the SEC tasks (QRScore-SEC)
and on the LongMemEval tasks (QRScore-LME) both drive accuracy to comparably
low levels by $K{=}32$, while the head sets detected on the Natural Questions
tasks (QRScore-NQ) remain within the confidence interval of the random
baseline through $K{=}16$.
 
At $K{=}16$, QRScore-SEC achieves 39.6\% accuracy ($95\%$ CI 32.8\%--46.4\%)
and QRScore-LME achieves 25.0\% (18.8\%--31.3\%); both are statistically
distinct from the random baseline of 88.2\% at the same $K$ (the unablated
baseline is 91.1\%). QRScore-NQ achieves 85.9\% (80.7\%--90.6\%) at $K{=}16$,
which overlaps with the random baseline; it does not separate from random
until $K{=}32$.
 
LongMemEval and our SEC benchmark share a structural property: each task is
posed over a single, continuous document. The Natural Questions detection
setup used in QRHead, by contrast, builds detection contexts from
concatenated, mutually disjoint Wikipedia passages. The head sets detected
under that setup are therefore optimized for inter-passage ranking rather
than intra-document span location. The transfer success of LME heads to SEC,
despite a large genre mismatch (chat dialogue vs.\ financial filings),
suggests that retrieval-head detection generalizes within a task paradigm but
not across paradigms.
